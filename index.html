Cost Price Model

What does this model measure?

This model calculates the full cost price per metric tonne (€/MT) for every finished product at Ecom — from raw beans through to powder and butter. It is built on SAP actuals, supplemented by direct measurement data (Fluke, sub-meters).

i
How to use this document: Use the navigation bar above to move between topics. The “Buckets A–D” section shows the cost build-up per department with allocation logic. “Model Decisions” covers the deliberate methodological choices and the reasoning behind them.

Model structure

The model follows the physical production chain: raw cocoa beans enter the Roasting department (3 lines) and are processed into liquor. Part of the liquor is sold directly; the rest goes to the Press room (7 presses), where it splits into cocoa butter and cake. The butter is deodorised in the Deso department; the cake is milled into powder.

Costs are calculated at each step and carried forward cumulatively. The cost price of a finished product always includes all conversion costs of every upstream step — Bulk Butter carries the full Roasting + Pressing + Deso cost stack; a powder product carries Roasting + Pressing + Powder costs.

The tabs in this document mirror that same structure. Each tab explains the why behind the model’s choices: how costs within a department are distributed across lines, recipes, or products — and what the reasoning was. Where relevant, the allocation logic is shown both at department level and at the level of individual lines or recipes within that department.

Cost behaviour

Every cost line in the model is classified by how it behaves relative to production volume. This classification determines how €/MT changes when output fluctuates.

Variable
Moves with output
Definition
Moves directly with volume or consumption. If production stops, cost stops.
Examples
Raw materials (beans, additives), direct gas per batch, direct electricity per batch, packaging
Implication
€/MT stays roughly constant regardless of monthly output volume
Semi-variable
Partly fixed, partly activity-driven
Definition
Has a fixed base component plus a variable component that responds to activity levels.
Examples
Indirect energy, water, labour (fixed crew + variable hours), maintenance (contracts + interventions)
Implication
€/MT rises when volume drops (fixed base spreads over fewer tonnes) and falls when volume rises
Fixed
Independent of volume
Definition
Contract- or CAPEX-driven. The cost is the same whether the line runs at 50% or 100% capacity.
Examples
Depreciation, Safety & Quality (audits, certification), cleaning contracts, lab costs
Implication
€/MT rises sharply when volume drops — main driver of cost variance in low-output months
Methodology

Allocation Methods

Every cost line in the model is allocated to a department and further to a product or recipe. This section documents exactly how — per department, per cost category. Select a department below.

i
Roasting (Branderij) — 3 lines, multiple recipes per line (Natural, Alkalised, Light Alk, High Alk). Cost allocation happens in two steps: first to the department as a whole, then within the department to lines and recipes.
RM
Raw Materials — Beans
100% direct to Roasting · Variable
+
Allocation to dept
100% to Roasting. No split needed — beans only enter at this stage.
Within dept
Bean cost × recipe tonnage share per line. Each recipe carries its proportional share of bean costs based on how many tonnes it produced.
Source
Actual bean invoices / spot market price from the Prices tab. Not pulled from SAP — entered directly based on purchase price.
GAS
Gas
Direct measurement + indirect residual · Variable / Semi-variable
+
Direct gas
Sub-meter per roasting batch. Actual consumption × gas price = direct cost. Pulled from Recipes Produced sheet column L. Within dept: per batch per line, then recipe tonnage share within line.
Indirect gas
SAP total gas (Scholt invoice) minus direct measured = residual. Between 70–80% of total factory gas consumption can be directly metered. The residual is what remains. Within dept: ÷3 over lines, then recipe tonnage share.
Indirect split
The residual is split: 25% by gas consumption share (the portion of the residual that is still actual factory consumption, allocated in line with measured usage) and 75% by tonnage (overhead costs embedded in the gas invoice: network charges, transport tariffs, taxes — these are not consumption-driven and should spread by volume).
Why this split
Of the unmeasured residual, approximately 25% is real gas burned in the factory in ways we cannot meter individually. The remaining ~75% is fixed cost embedded in the invoice (network fees, capacity tariffs, energy taxes) that has no relationship to how much any department consumed. Allocating that 75% by consumption would unfairly penalise high-intensity departments for a cost that is independent of their usage.
ELE
Electricity
Direct measurement + indirect residual · Variable / Semi-variable
+
Direct electricity
Smart meter per roasting line per batch. Actual kWh × electricity price. Pulled from Recipes Produced sheet column J. Within dept: per line per batch → recipe tonnage share within line.
Indirect electricity
SAP total electricity minus directly metered = residual. Between 70–80% of total factory electricity can be directly measured. What remains is the indirect pool. Roasting receives ~44.3% of this pool. Within dept: ÷ 3 (equal split over lines) → then by recipe tonnage share within each line.
Indirect split
The residual is split: 20% by electricity consumption share (the portion of the residual that is still actual factory consumption, allocated in line with measured usage) and 80% by tonnage (network tariffs, capacity charges, energy taxes — fixed costs embedded in the invoice that have no relationship to how much any department consumed).
Why this split
Of the unmeasured residual, approximately 20% is real electricity consumed in parts of the factory we cannot individually meter. The remaining ~80% is fixed cost in the invoice (network fees, capacity tariffs, taxes) with no relationship to actual usage per department. Allocating that 80% by consumption would unfairly penalise high-intensity departments for a cost they did not drive.

Pro-rata by recipe tonnage. All recipes within a line carry the same €/MT for water.

LBR
Labour
Equal split over lines + recipe tonnage · Semi-variable
+
Melting step
Melting labour allocated first, pro-rata by tonnage share of total Roasting throughput.
Lines 1, 2, 3
Remaining labour split equally across 3 lines (÷3), then within each line by recipe tonnage share.
Why ÷3
Operators run all 3 lines simultaneously. No hour registration per line exists. Equal split avoids penalising lines running lower-throughput recipes that are not less labour-intensive.
Includes
Permanent salaries (gross + holiday pay + pension + AWf + 13th month) + contract / agency workers. All Payroll SAP lines are aggregated before splitting.
Indirect labour costs
Costs linked to people rather than production (travel, training, canteen, car leasing, HR overhead — see General Overhead card) are allocated by direct labour share per department. Roasting receives ~48% of this pool, reflecting its share of total direct labour hours across the factory.

Maintenance & Spare Parts
Direct per line + indirect pool · Semi-variable
+
Direct maintenance
WE-postings per purchase order in SAP, attributable to a specific line. Split within the line by recipe tonnage share.
Indirect maintenance & spare parts
Covers two types of cost: (1) small consumable items (tape, screws, generic supplies) that cannot be attributed to a specific line and are split equally across all lines within the department; (2) spare parts and maintenance orders where the destination department is unknown. Press Room (Perserij) — 7 presses, one pressing step produces two co-products (butter and cake). Allocation within the department happens per press; the co-product cost split uses market value allocation.

Raw Material — Liquor
Carried forward from Roasting · Variable
+
Method
The weighted average liquor cost price from Roasting is carried forward as the raw material cost for the Press Room. No new allocation needed — this is a cumulative step.

After pressing, the liquor input cost is split between butter and cake using market value allocation: each co-product carries input cost proportional to its market value per tonne.

MV
Market Value Allocation — Butter vs Cake
Co-product split · Core model decision
+
What it is
During pressing, butter and cake are produced simultaneously in a single step — there is one press, one set of costs. Those costs cannot be physically separated. Market value allocation is the method used to determine how much of those shared pressing costs each co-product will carry forward when they eventually go their separate ways.
When the split takes effect
Inside the Press Room, butter and cake are still treated as one combined output. The split only becomes visible when the products physically separate: butter leaves for Deso, cake leaves for the Powder department. From that point onwards, each product carries its allocated share of pressing costs as part of its raw material input cost for the next step.
How the ratio is determined
Each co-product carries pressing costs in proportion to its relative market value per tonne. Butter, being significantly more valuable than cake, absorbs a proportionally larger share of the pressing cost.
Why not weight (55/45)
Weight allocation would make cake artificially expensive and butter artificially cheap. Market value reflects the economic contribution of each product — the industry standard approach for co-products of unequal value.
Market prices
Reference prices for butter and cake sourced from the Prices tab. Updated monthly with market data.

Where the numbers come from

Two source systems feed the model: SAP for financial cost data, and SmartReact for all production and energy data. This section documents which table or export is used for each input.
